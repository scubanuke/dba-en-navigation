AI GOVERNANCE

AI Governance Design Basis — Energy Sector

The DBA-EN series is the first infrastructure design basis framework in any sector to require a formal AI governance design basis for every facility class across all three sub-sectors. The framework addresses the full AI control plane: deterministic ML systems operating autonomously below the Bright Line; Gen AI advisory tools requiring human Command Broker oversight above it; the optimization-safety boundary where commercial process optimization erodes safety margins; the inventory depletion attack surface in automated scheduling systems; and the supply chain trustworthiness precondition for inverter-based generation resources. The Command Broker architecture is the enforcement mechanism at the Bright Line — one part of a governance framework that was built into the series from the beginning, not added reactively.

Governance PrincipleWhere EstablishedWhat It Requires
The Bright Line DBA-DC v1.6 Formal boundary between autonomous ML (deterministic, formally testable — permitted below) and Gen AI (non-deterministic — Command Broker required above). Applied to every facility class in the series.
Optimization-safety boundary DBA-GAS-FC2 In continuous-process facilities, the Bright Line must constrain the plant's own commercial optimization logic — not only adversarial manipulation. Advanced Process Control (APC) and Real-Time Optimization (RTO) envelopes must include safety margin setbacks from SIS trip points, enforced by architecture. Series first.
Inventory depletion attack surface DBA-GAS-FC3 / FC7 ML scheduling systems governing storage inventory and automated dispatch are simultaneously the attack surface for undetectable resource depletion. Cumulative pattern analysis — not just individual transaction authorization — is a security requirement.
Firmware trustworthiness as Bright Line precondition DBA-ES-GN-FC8 An inverter operating on unverified firmware cannot be treated as a below-Bright-Line autonomous ML system regardless of apparent normal behavior. Independent firmware verification is a precondition for autonomous IBR operation. Applies to ~80–85% of deployed U.S. utility inverters by manufacturer origin. Series first.
